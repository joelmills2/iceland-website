Abstract

This website provides an in-depth exploration of the geology of Iceland, a land shaped by the forces of tectonic plates, volcanoes, and glaciers. Designed for the final project of the GEOL104 course at Queen’s University, this website offers a comprehensive introduction to the geological features and processes that have shaped Iceland’s landscape.

The website begins by examining the plate tectonic forces that created Iceland, the Mid-Atlantic Ridge that runs through the country, and the stationary hotspot beneath the ridge. It then explores Iceland’s diverse array of volcanoes, discussing the volcanic structures as well as eruption types.

We then look at Iceland’s glaciers, and discuss the wondrous interactions between glaciers and volcanoes, which can lead to mass glacial flooding. We also consider the role that glaciers play in shaping Iceland's landscape, and the threats they face from climate change.

Finally, we explore the geological history of iceland, where we learn how Iceland was formed with a focus on the country’s glacial history. Throughout the website, images, diagrams and figures are used to help illustrate key concepts and geological processes. By the end of this website, users will have gained a thorough understanding of the unique geology of iceland, and how it can inform our understanding of geology more broadly.

Plate Tectonics

The geology of Iceland is one of a kind, displaying various unique features and landforms that can only be found together because of Iceland’s plate tectonics. Iceland sits above the Mid-Atlantic Ridge, a giant rift valley and a mountain range that separates the Eurasian and North American plates5. The Mid-Atlantic Ridge is famous as its discovery led to the development and widespread recognition of the theory of seafloor spreading6. Iceland also lays on top of the much smaller and less significant Greenland-Iceland-Faroe Islands ridge5. These ridges, along with the presence of a stationary hotspot beneath Iceland, are the cause of Iceland’s wondrous landforms and extreme seismic and volcanic activity5.

The location of Iceland on the Mid-Atlantic Ridge, which represents a divergent plate boundary, results in the separation of Iceland into two distinct tectonic plates; the Eurasian plate to the east and the North American plate to the west5. Each year, the plates diverge approximately 19 mm from one another7, expanding Iceland at half the rate of a fingernail. The consistent formation of new crust at the ridge has resulted in Iceland’s rocks being relatively young at 25 million years old5. The superimposition of the ridge and hotspot generates a diverse range of geological features, including various faults, folds, and intrusions that characterize different regions of Iceland.

Divergent plate boundaries are known for their oblique nature, resulting in normal faulting and diking along the ridge, particularly in volcanic systems like the Northern Volcanic Zone7. These volcanic systems frequently display gradual synforms that incline strata towards the rift zone7, while an anticlinal formation can be found between the Eastern and Western Volcanic Zones5. Along with volcanism, divergent boundaries are also associated with major seismic activity due to faulting7. The valley separating the two diverging plates is a deformation zone and experiences various crustal movements along its axis7. In the South Iceland Seismic Zone, high-pressure strike-slip faults are common and can result in earthquakes ranging up to magnitude 77. In the northern part of the deformation zone, strike-slip transform faults occur roughly perpendicular to the ridge in a manner similar to tilting books on a bookshelf, giving rise to the term "bookshelf faulting"7. The Icelandic volcanic zones exhibit these features to varying degrees, with some being inactive volcanoes displaying significant faulting, while others are simply mounds of volcanic material due to frequent eruptions7.

Iceland's unique location atop a mantle plume and a diverging boundary has resulted in the formation of remarkable geological structures that cannot be found anywhere else in the world, making it a fascinating and unique destination for geologists and travellers alike.

Volcanoes

Iceland’s location on the Mid-Atlantic Ridge along with the hotspot located below it, make it one of the most volcanically active places on Earth7. Iceland's volcanic activity has had a profound impact on its landscape, shaping its rugged mountains, lava fields, and hot springs, and creating some of the most dramatic and awe-inspiring natural wonders in the world.

Iceland’s 30 volcanic systems8 cover roughly one-third of the entire landmass and can be categorized into 5 region-specific groups9. The Icelandic volcanic belts comprise 11 volcanoes, while the volcanic zones contain 19. Six are located in the Western Volcanic Zone, five in the Northern Volcanic Zone, three in the Reykjanes Volcanic Zone, and eight in the Eastern Volcanic Zone8. Since the tectonic features in each of these zones differ, Iceland’s volcanoes fit into a diverse array of structural classifications, from simple fissures to massive shield volcanoes9. Just 17% of Iceland’s volcanoes fit into the classification of a shield volcano, and many share characteristics of both shield and stratovolcanoes9. Iceland’s stratovolcanoes are topped with cone-shaped calderas or craters9 Iceland's volcanic landscape can be characterized by long rows of cone-shaped volcanoes; these basalt volcanoes line tunnels through the subterranean expanse of Iceland and erupt with varying degrees of explosiveness.

Just as the structures of Iceland’s volcanoes vary, so too do the sizes and explosiveness of their eruptions. The volcanic eruptions in Iceland can be effusive, explosive, or both10. Of the recorded eruptions, 78% have been explosive, while 9% have been effusive, and 13% have been a combination of the two9. The explosive eruptions can be further classified as wet or dry eruptions, with subtypes of Surtseyan, Phreatoplinian, and Plinian eruptions9. Due to the abundance of glaciers and volcanoes in Iceland, it is common for these landforms to intersect, leading to volcanic eruptions beneath glaciers8. Consequently, these interactions can result in the melting of large portions of ice and may generate mass flooding8.

Among the four volcanic zones, the Eastern Volcanic Zone (EVZ) is the most active zone by a considerable margin. Out of the 205 recorded eruptions, approximately 80% have occurred in this zone9. Furthermore, of the 9 volcanoes in this zone 8 have been active9. The EVZ also contains four active volcanoes: the Grímsvötn, Veidivötn, Hekla and Katla volcanoes. On the other hand, the Northern Volcanic Zone has had only three of its five volcanoes active, while the Western and Reykjanes Volcanic Zones have had just four of their nine volcanoes active in the past 1100 years9.

In contemporary times, the Icelandic volcanoes have garnered attention as fascinating marvels, rather than as hazardous natural phenomena. This is the case because the frequency of fatalities caused by these volcanoes is exceedingly low, and the deposition of tephra typically results in negligible harm, as the proximate regions are frequently uninhabited8.

Iceland's volcanic activity is a unique and awe-inspiring natural wonder, and with a diverse range of volcanoes and eruptions, their volcanoes attract thousands of visitors and geologists to Iceland each year.

Glaciers

Glaciers are a fundamental aspect of Iceland’s natural beauty and wonder. Glaciers are generally deemed inactive as they move at a rate that is too slow to be observed; however, Icelandic glaciers display an exceptional phenomenon as they incur sudden movements called surges. Despite their vast size, Icelandic glaciers have gradually diminished over time. Just 200 years ago, glaciers and sea ice had covered and surrounded practically all of Iceland’s land mass6, whereas presently they cover roughly 10% of the island5. While geologists typically investigate the annual variations in the glaciers' dimensions caused by temperature fluctuations, it is the surges that distinguish Icelandic glaciers from one another and other glaciers in the world.

Glacier surges are events characterized by a sudden increase in glacier velocity due to an upsurge in meltwater below5. Surge-type glaciers are a characteristic of Icelandic glaciers as there are 26 of them ranging in size from 0.5km to 1500km5. Surge-type glaciers are especially common in major ice caps due to the presence of volcanic systems in Iceland10. The abundance of volcanoes and glaciers in Iceland leads to dangerous interactions between the two landforms. Many active volcanoes form below glaciers, and eruptions lead to rapid melting and subsequent surges6. Surges have numerous effects on Iceland's landforms and the environment. Surges affect the hydrology of Iceland as they increase the mass of the glacier and by extension, the distance they are pushed10. Furthermore, surges can cause massive flooding events called Jökulhlaup when a well of meltwater breaks out from below the glacier6. These effects pose significant environmental hazards to local populations6. Although surges can be hazardous when active, long-term observations reveal that surges occur at regular intervals depending on the properties of the glacier and their environment, enabling geologists to predict these events and respond accordingly10. Surge glaciers also possess a very shallow slope when compared to other glacier types at just 1.6 to 4 degrees10. The most notable example of a surge-type glacier in Iceland is the Vatnajökull ice cap. This glacier is the largest ice cap in Iceland, and due to its location above a volcanic system, it incurs a multitude of surges10. Other Icelandic glaciers tend to follow similar trends to those of other glaciers around the world; they experience an annual advance and retreat and undergo similar long-term changes in mass.

Icelandic glaciers are unique due to the prominence of surge-type glaciers and their interactions with volcanic systems. Due to these unique processes glaciers are a fundamental aspect of Iceland’s natural awe and beauty and are considered a requisite destination for tourists and geologists alike.

Geological History

Despite its remarkable geological diversity, Iceland is relatively young in geological terms. The island's origins can be traced back approximately 60 million years ago when submarine tectonic and volcanic activity resulted in the formation of a volcanic arc that extended from Canada to Scotland5. Within the past 25 million years, a stationary hotspot located on the Mid-Atlantic Ridge beneath Iceland caused the crust to bulge out above sea level5, transforming a submarine plateau into a viable landform6, 11. Geologists first discovered the hotspot in Iceland due to the island's highly irregular volcanic activity5. Although volcanic activity from the hotspot continues to the present day, it has reduced in scale significantly since its inception 60 million years ago and is no longer capable of creating new landforms11. Currently, the oldest rocks exposed on Iceland’s surface range from 14 to 16 million years old11.

In recent times, Iceland has experienced significant glacial activity, with 22 distinct glacial and interglacial cycles occurring over the past 3 million years12. These cycles correspond to periods of substantial glacial expansion and subsequent retreat, caused by fluctuations in global temperature12. The initial glacial foundational phase was initiated approximately 4 million years ago and was followed by a transitional growth phase, resulting in a unified Icelandic ice sheet covering the Island5. Over the next million years, this ice sheet fragmented and melted, leaving behind smaller ice deposits across the country5.

The most recent major glacial event in Iceland is the Little Ice Age, a global cooling phenomenon that took place between the 1300s and mid-1800s5. As average temperatures dropped, glaciers significantly expanded in mass, and sea ice surrounded Iceland6. This was the last major glacial event in the island’s history.

Iceland is characterized by numerous natural phenomena that can be attributed to its distinctive origins. Due to Iceland’s relatively youthful geological history, geologists and historians alike have had the opportunity to thoroughly investigate and study its origins.
